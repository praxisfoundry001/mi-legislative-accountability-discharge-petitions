# Discharge Petitions & Legislative Accountability in Michigan

**A Multi-Trigger Constitutional Calendaring Architecture**

## What is this?

This repository contains a model Michigan constitutional and legislative-procedure proposal designed to make introduced bills receive a timely recorded floor vote when enough public or local-government support is shown.

The proposal uses four independent routes:

1. citizen petitions;
2. municipal resolutions;
3. school district / intermediate school district resolutions;
4. county commission resolutions.

It also includes a voluntary compact-based channel for Tribal Nation participation and advisory public posting.

## Why should I care?

Many democratic systems allow bills to be introduced but then buried without a recorded vote. That can make public accountability hard to see.

This model responds to that problem by separating two things:

**A public right to force a vote**
from
**a legislature’s right to vote yes or no.**

The proposal does not force passage. It forces calendaring, debate, transparency, and a recorded vote.

## What is the claim?

The core claim is that legislative accountability can be improved by creating lawful public triggers that move a qualifying bill from blockage to recorded consideration.

The model uses a simple architecture:

**public trigger → verification → calendared vote → public accountability**

It is written for Michigan, but the design pattern may be useful anywhere a state, local government, institution, association, union, board, or membership body has something like discharge petitions, agenda-setting petitions, member-triggered votes, or formal calendaring rules.

## What is this not?

This is not current Michigan law.

It is not legal advice.

It does not guarantee that the same text will work in every state or institution.

It does not require legislators to pass a bill. It only creates a pathway to require a timely recorded vote once a qualifying trigger is satisfied.

Any state or institutional adaptation should be reviewed against that jurisdiction’s constitution, statutes, rules, petition procedures, ballot-initiative law, sovereignty obligations, and parliamentary structure.

## How was this done?

The proposal builds a model calendaring system around independent trigger routes, public verification, anti-delay rules, transparency requirements, funding disclosure, follows-intent review, and severability.

Michigan is used as the worked example. The deeper transferable idea is the architecture, not the state-specific citations.

## Where do I go next?

Use the Michigan draft as a pattern.

A person adapting it elsewhere should ask:

1. What body has the power to bury proposals?
2. What legal or procedural mechanism can force agenda access?
3. Who should be allowed to trigger a vote?
4. What threshold is high enough to prevent abuse but low enough to preserve public accountability?
5. Who verifies the trigger?
6. What deadline prevents delay?
7. What happens if the official responsible refuses to calendar the item?
8. How does the system preserve debate and amendment without allowing obstruction?
